Module 10 — Lesson 02

Agent UX & Conversation Design

A data agent is only useful if non-technical users can interact with it naturally. Design for store managers, not data analysts.

AGENT UX — PROGRESSIVE DISCLOSURE
The Problem

DataPulse Is Accurate. Nobody Uses It.

After Priya Sharma's semantic layer fixed the $3.4M revenue discrepancy, DataPulse was producing consistent, trusted numbers. Raj Patel celebrated. But three weeks later, Lena Park, AcmeCorp's Product Manager, pulled up the usage dashboards and saw a problem no one expected: only 23 of 200 store managers were using DataPulse regularly. The accuracy fix hadn't moved adoption at all.

Lena decided to find out why. She called Sofia Torres at Store #47 — one of the 23 active users — and three managers who had stopped using it. The feedback was brutal. One manager said he asked "how are we doing?" and DataPulse returned a SQL error. Another said it showed her a table with 47 columns when she just wanted a single number. A third said he asked about "last quarter" and DataPulse used calendar quarters (April–June) instead of AcmeCorp's fiscal quarters (July–September), giving him completely wrong numbers.

Lena's Diagnosis
The problem was not accuracy — Priya had solved that. The problem was user experience. DataPulse was designed by engineers for engineers. It didn't clarify ambiguity, it didn't format results for non-technical users, and it communicated errors in raw PostgreSQL error codes instead of plain English. Lena told Raj: "We built a brilliant engine and put it behind a terrible steering wheel. I need to redesign how DataPulse talks to people."
Principle

Designing for Non-Technical Users

Lena's first principle was simple: DataPulse's primary users are not writing SQL today and do not want to start. They are store managers like Sofia checking daily sales, regional directors comparing performance, and executives like the CEO looking for trends. The agent must feel like talking to a knowledgeable colleague, not like operating a database terminal. Lena wrote three design rules and pinned them to the DataPulse team's Slack channel.

01
Plain Language

Never expose SQL, table names, or error codes directly. Translate everything into business language the user already knows. Sofia should never see fact_sales or SQLSTATE.

02
Assumed Context

If Sofia at Store #47 asks a question, scope the answer to her store by default. Do not force her to specify what DataPulse already knows about her — her store, her region (Southwest), her role.

03
Graceful Limits

When DataPulse cannot answer, explain what it can do instead. Never return a blank response or a raw error traceback. The manager who got a SQL error should have gotten a helpful explanation.

Pattern

Clarification Dialogs

Ambiguous questions are inevitable — as the manager who typed "how are we doing?" demonstrated. Lena's redesign ensured DataPulse does not guess silently. Instead, it asks a targeted clarification question before proceeding. The goal is to ask the minimum needed to disambiguate, not to interrogate the user. Lena tested every clarification flow with Sofia Torres, who became her go-to beta tester.

DataPulse — Clarification Example
What was revenue in Q3?
Just to confirm — by Q3, do you mean AcmeCorp's fiscal Q3 (Jul–Sep 2025) or fiscal Q3 (Jul–Sep 2024)?
FY25 Q3 FY24 Q3
FY25 Q3
Revenue for fiscal Q3 2025 (Jul–Sep) was $43.8M, up 8% from fiscal Q3 2024. Want me to break this down by region?
Design Rule
Lena insisted on clickable options whenever possible (buttons, quick replies). Sofia pointed out that most store managers check DataPulse on their phones while walking the floor — typing is slower and more error-prone on mobile. Lena also enforced a strict rule: one clarification question at a time. Cascading questions feel like an interrogation. The manager who typed "last quarter" should have been offered two buttons, not a paragraph asking him to specify fiscal year, quarter number, and metric type.
Pattern

Progressive Disclosure

The manager who received a 47-column table illustrated another problem: DataPulse was dumping everything it knew into one response. Lena's fix was progressive disclosure — starting with a headline answer and offering drill-down options on demand. She designed four levels, inspired by how Sofia Torres naturally asked questions: first the headline, then the context, then the detail, then the raw data.

Level 1: Headline

"Revenue this month is $3.2M, up 5% vs. last month."

Single number with context. Answers Sofia's question in one sentence while she's walking the store floor.

Level 2: Summary Card

Key metrics grouped into a compact card: revenue, order count, average order value, top category.

Offered with "Want to see the full breakdown?" — only when Sofia taps for more.

Level 3: Drill-Down

Detailed table or chart: revenue by store, by category, by week. Includes comparisons and trends.

Only shown when explicitly requested. This is what Raj and Derek need, not Sofia.

Level 4: Raw Data

Exportable CSV or the underlying SQL query for analysts who want to verify or extend the analysis.

Available via "Export data" or "Show query" links. Maya Chen uses this level to audit DataPulse's SQL.

Pattern

Error Communication

The manager who received a SQL error instead of an answer became Lena's canonical example of what not to do. When something goes wrong, DataPulse must communicate the issue without jargon. A store manager should never see "NullPointerException" or "timeout on connection pool." Instead, errors should explain what happened, why, and what the user can do.

Before: What a Manager Actually Saw

ERROR: relation 'fact_sales' partition 'p_20260324' does not exist

Exposes infrastructure details. The manager had no idea what to do and stopped using DataPulse entirely.

After: Lena's Redesigned Error

"I couldn't find sales data for yesterday — the data warehouse updates at 6 AM, and yesterday's data isn't available yet. Want me to show you the most recent numbers I have?"

Explains the situation, sets expectations, offers an alternative. The manager stays engaged.

Lena's Error Communication Patterns for DataPulse
  • Data freshness issue: "The sales data I have is from yesterday evening. Today's data hasn't loaded yet — AcmeCorp's warehouse updates at 6 AM. Want me to answer with yesterday's numbers?"
  • Permission denied: "I don't have access to individual employee records. I can show you department-level summaries. Would that help?" (Marcus Webb's access controls in action.)
  • Ambiguous query: "I found several metrics that could match your question. Could you clarify which one you mean?" (With clickable options, per Lena's rule.)
  • No data found: "There are no sales recorded for Store #203 in December. This could mean the store was closed or the data hasn't been imported yet. Want me to check the data team?"
Output Design

Visualization Recommendations

Lena worked with Sofia to define how DataPulse should choose the right output format based on the data shape and the user's likely intent. A single number does not need a chart. A comparison across 50 stores does not belong in a paragraph. Sofia's feedback was direct: "When I ask how my store is doing, I want a number with an arrow. When I ask how I compare to other Southwest stores, I want a ranked list."

Data Shape Best Format AcmeCorp Example
Single value Single number with context "Store #47 Revenue: $142K (+8%)"
2–5 values Summary card / bullet list Top 3 Southwest stores by revenue
Time series Line chart Store #47 weekly revenue over fiscal Q3
Category comparison Bar chart Revenue by product category at Store #47
Proportions Pie / donut chart Revenue split by channel (in-store vs. online)
Detailed records Table with sorting All 200 stores ranked by performance for Derek's report
Formatting Best Practices
Lena codified these rules into DataPulse's output layer: use headers to organize multi-part answers. Use bold for key numbers. Use bullet points for lists of 3+ items. Always include comparison context (vs. last month, vs. goal, vs. average). Highlight numbers that are significantly above or below expectations — Sofia wants to know at a glance if something needs her attention.
Case Study

AcmeCorp: Sofia Tests Lena's Redesign

Lena shipped the UX redesign and asked Sofia Torres to be the first tester. Sofia opened DataPulse on her phone at Store #47 and typed the exact question that had originally returned a SQL error for another manager: the vaguest possible question.

The Results of Lena's Redesign
DataPulse asked one clarification question (store vs. region), used Sofia's known context (Store #47, Southwest), returned a summary card with key metrics, highlighted direction (up/down), and offered drill-down options. The entire interaction took under 10 seconds and required no technical knowledge. Sofia texted Lena: "This is what it should have been from the start." Within six weeks of the UX redesign, adoption jumped from 23 to 124 store managers. Raj showed the numbers to the CEO, who said: "Now it's not just accurate — it's actually useful." Lena's work proved that for DataPulse, UX was not a nice-to-have. It was the difference between a tool that 12% of AcmeCorp used and one that 62% used.
